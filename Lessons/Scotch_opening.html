The Scotch

Main Lines

This is the opening position:

The basic idea is to gain control of the center of the board by trading white’s d pawn for black’s e pawn. Black’s best move in this position is 3... exd4 (which you will see a majority of the time). If black plays something worse like 4… d6, then 5. dxe4 leads to a better position for white by either going up a pawn (if 5... dxe4 6. Qxd8 Nxd8 7. Nxe5), or disallowing black to castle after 5...dxe4 6. Qxd8 Kxd8 (white should win after this sequence as well). Sometimes black will also decide to immediately trade pawns and knights. This leaves the white Queen on d4 along with e4, and black has none of their pieces developed (a good start for white).

So if black knows the Scotch, they will most likely play the best move 3… exd4. And as white, I recommend 4. Nxd4. There are other variations and gambits that can be played, but oftentimes white can gain a better advantage by sticking to this main line. Be aware that this opening can progress into many different lines.

Ok, here we are:

From this position, black has a few different options. Let’s break down some of the most common moves.

Classic

4. Nxd4 Bc5

5. Nxc6 Qf6

With the black Queen on f6, white is forced to prevent checkmate on f2. So, the Knight on c6 can be recaptured after a move like 6. Qd2 or Qf3. Play usually continues with 6...dxc6 7. Nc3. I like playing this position as white because there are 4 v 3 king pawns, and black’s queen pawns are doubled for now. After developing the rest of my pieces and castling, I have a solid position with my e pawn still covering center squares.

Other classic lines include:

5. Be3

This is usually followed by 5...Qf6 6. c3 Nge7 7. Bc4

5. Nb3 (this is playable, but I have not found that it leads to any advantage as white after 5...Bb6).

Steinitz

This variation is not as common, but you should know the lines if black decides to go for it.

4. Nxc4 Qh4.

This threatens mate on f2, while also threatening e4.

If you run into Qh4, then play this if black goes along: 5. Nc3 Bb4 6. Be2 Qxe4 7. Nb5 Bxc3+ 8. bxc3 Kd8 9. 0-0.

You are then down a pawn, but the black king is moved out of position, you are castled, and black is underdeveloped.

Schmidt

The lines:

1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 Nc6 3. d4 exd4 4. Nxd4 Nc6

White should play Nc3 to defend e4. But expect to have the knight pinned down with Bb4. Trade off the knights and put your bishop on d3.

Scotch Trap

In blitz games (maybe against lower rated players), try this move out to see if black falls into a beautiful forced mate sequence: 1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 Nc6 3. d4 exd4 4. Ng5

If black plays a couple of terrible moves here, you have the game won. (?? indicates a blunder).
